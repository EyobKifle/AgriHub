Soil Preparation

Good soil is the start of a good harvest. Learn how to prepare your land.

Image: Healthy, dark soil

Why is Good Soil Important?

Think of soil as the house for your crops. A good house protects and feeds. Good soil gives your plants the food (nutrients), water, and air they need to grow strong and healthy. If your soil is poor, your crops will be weak and your harvest small.

Diagram: Steps of ploughing and adding manure

Practical Steps for Soil Preparation

  1. Clear the Land: Remove all weeds, old plants, and large stones from your field. Weeds steal food and water from your crops.
  2. Plough the Soil: Use an ox-plough (maresha) or a hoe to turn the soil. Ploughing breaks up hard soil, kills pests, and lets air and water enter. Plough at least 20-30 cm deep (about the length of your hand to your elbow).
  3. Add Manure or Compost: After ploughing, spread animal manure or compost over the soil. This adds important nutrients. For a field the size of a small house, you might need 5-10 wheelbarrows of manure. Mix it into the top layer of the soil.
  4. Level the Field: Make the ground flat and even. This helps water spread evenly and stops soil from washing away during heavy rain. You can use a wooden plank pulled by oxen or a rake for this.
  5. Make Furrows or Beds: For crops like maize or potatoes, make raised rows (furrows). This helps with irrigation and drainage. For vegetables like onions or carrots, you can make raised flat areas (beds).

Local Tips for Ethiopian Farmers

  • Timing is Key: In the highlands, start preparing your land after the first small rains (belg) begin, so the soil is soft enough to plough.
  • Know Your Soil: Red soils (common in many areas) can be good but may need more manure. Black, heavy soils hold water well but can be hard to work when dry.
  • Crop Rotation: Do not plant the same crop (like teff or maize) in the same field every single year. Change it. Plant beans or peas one year, then maize the next. This keeps the soil healthy.
